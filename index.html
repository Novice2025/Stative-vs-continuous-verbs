Stative Verbs: The logical divide

English Brains vs. French Brains: A Deep Dive

Daby method

1. Big picture: Different brains

English: State vs. Action

  • State Verbs: Mental states (= know, believe), emotions (= like, love), possession (= have), senses (= see, hear). Always simple present.


  • Action Verbs: Physical actions (run, eat) and dynamic processes (grow, change). Can be simple or continuous.

English forces a choice:

  • State verbs use simple tense only
  • Action verbs can use simple or continuous/progressive

    • French: The Présent de l'indicatif - Master

    French relies on the present for both. "Être en train de" is only for ACTIONS.

    2. The Deep Dive: Classification

    Mental states

    Know, believe, understand, remember.



    French uses simple present for all. "Je suis en train de comprendre" is unnatural.



    French uses simple present for all. "Je suis en train de croire" is unnatural.

    Emotional states

    Like, love, hate, want, need



    Informal English:
    "I'm loving it" is stylistic; French stays simple (J'adore).


    Possession

    Own, belong, contain, have.


    Stative:
    Simple present only.

    Senses & Description

    See, hear, seem, remain, exist.

    Distinction depends on whether it's a state or active sniffing/tasting.

    4. When does English use -ing with verbs that are normally stative, because they become actions, but in French we still use the simple present?

    verbsverbsverbs
    thinkhavesee
    feellooktaste
    smellbeappear


    In English, these can be used in continuous form to indicate a temporary state or an action in progress. In French, they remain in the simple present regardless of context.



    For example,
    "I am thinking" (temporary state) vs. "Je pense" (simple present).



    "I am having a good time" vs. "Je passe un bon moment."



    "I am seeing a doctor" vs. "Je vois un médecin."



    This highlights the fundamental difference in how English and French handle stative verbs and their continuous forms.

    Shortcut 1: Default to French present tense

    • Anytime you see am/is/are + -ing in English, ask: Is it happening now?
    • If yes → use French present tense
    • I’m studying → J’étudie
    • I’m cooking → Je cuisine
    • I’m reading → Je lis

    Use “être en train de” only for emphasis: “in the middle of doing it”.

    Shortcut 2: Stative verbs → simple present

    No continuous form in French for these verbs:

    • know → Je sais
    • like → J’aime
    • love → J’adore
    • want → Je veux
    • need → J’ai besoin de
    • understand → Je comprends

    No continuous. No drama.

    Shortcut 3: Behavior vs character

    • You are rude → Tu es malpoli (character)
    • You are being rude → Tu te comportes mal

    French does not use a progressive form — it changes wording instead.

    Shortcut 4: One verb, multiple meanings

    English splits meanings. French uses different verbs:

    • think → penser / réfléchir
    • see → voir / rencontrer
    • have → avoir / faire l’expérience de
    • feel → se sentir / toucher
    • look → avoir l’air / regarder

    Train your brain: meaning → verb, not word → word.

    4. Practical habits for learners

    • Default to Présent: If in doubt, use French present simple.
      ==> "Être en train de" is only for emphatic "in the middle of" actions.
    • Don't map -ing to "en train de" for mental states: "Je suis en train d'adorer" is unnatural.
    • Behavior vs Character: English uses "You are being [rude]" for temporary behavior. French prefers "Tu es [impoli] aujourd'hui" or "Tu te comportes [mal]".
